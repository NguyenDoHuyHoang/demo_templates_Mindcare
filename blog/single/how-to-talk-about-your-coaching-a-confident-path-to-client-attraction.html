How to Talk About Your Coaching: A Confident Path to Client Attraction

If you’re a certified coach, you likely started your journey with passion, purpose, and a desire to make a difference. But there’s one hurdle that continues to trip up even the most skilled professionals: knowing how to talk about your coaching.

You’re not alone. Many qualified coaches find themselves tongue-tied when it comes to explaining what they do. Why? Because coaching is deeply personal, often transformational, and hard to distill into a simple, catchy pitch. And if you struggle to communicate your value, it becomes increasingly difficult to attract clients who truly need your services.

In this blog, we’ll explore how to overcome this common roadblock and speak about your coaching in a way that connects, inspires, and converts. Plus, we’ll introduce you to a game-changing solution—Love2Coach—that helps you confidently showcase your coaching without pressure, complexity, or costly marketing tactics.

Why It’s Hard to Talk About Coaching

Let’s address the elephant in the room: coaching isn’t a product you can hold in your hands. You’re selling transformation, not a tangible item. That alone makes explaining your offer feel abstract. Add to that:

  • Fear of sounding salesy or pushy

  • Lack of clarity about your niche or offer

  • Worry that you’re not saying “the right thing”

  • Feeling like your impact is hard to quantify

And suddenly, even talking to a friend about your business becomes uncomfortable.

But here’s the truth: If you don’t talk about your coaching confidently and clearly, people won’t know how you can help them. They won’t understand your value—and that means they won’t become your clients.

Step 1: Clarify Your Niche and Ideal Client

Before you can talk about your coaching, you need to be clear on who you help and what you help them with. A strong niche provides focus and makes your messaging more compelling.

Ask yourself:

  • Who do I serve best?

  • What problems do they face?

  • What outcome or transformation do they want?

Example: Instead of saying “I help people feel better,” you might say, “I help mid-career professionals overcome burnout and find renewed purpose in their work.”

The more specific you are, the easier it becomes to speak with authority and authenticity.

Step 2: Find Your Core Message

Once you’ve nailed down your niche, it’s time to craft a core message. This is the central story of your coaching practice. Think of it as your go-to statement when someone asks, “What do you do?”

Your message should include:

  • Who you help

  • The problem they face

  • The solution you provide

  • The result they can expect

Example: “I’m a mindset coach who works with ambitious women dealing with imposter syndrome. I help them rewire their thinking so they can step into leadership roles with confidence.”

Step 3: Use Everyday Language

Many coaches fall into the trap of using abstract, high-level language. But most people aren’t familiar with terms like “transformational growth” or “energetic alignment.” Keep it real.

Instead:

  • Use phrases your clients would use

  • Describe results they can relate to

  • Share stories or examples that show the impact

Example: “My client used to dread going to work. Now, she’s excited to lead meetings and just got promoted.”

When people see themselves in your stories, they connect with your offer.

Step 4: Practice With Confidence

Talking about your coaching is a skill—and like any skill, it takes practice.

Try this:

  • Record yourself describing your coaching in 30 seconds

  • Practice your message on friends or fellow coaches

  • Roleplay discovery calls to get more comfortable

The more you talk about your work, the more natural it becomes. Repetition builds clarity and confidence.
